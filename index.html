2×2 · Beginner

Solving the Pocket Cube

The 2×2 has no center or edge pieces — only 8 corners. That makes it simpler than the 3×3 but the same core ideas apply. You'll learn to solve it layer by layer.

Step 01
Solve the First Layer
Pick any color to be your bottom face. Find one corner of that color and bring it to the bottom-right-front position. Use R U R' U' repeated until it drops in correctly. Repeat for all 4 bottom corners.

Tip: Don't worry about the top layer yet — just get all 4 bottom corners solved with correct color orientation.
Step 02
Position the Top Corners
Hold the solved layer on the bottom. Look at the top layer — you need to get all 4 corners into their correct positions (matching the side colors) even if they're twisted.

Use U moves to find a corner that's already in the right position, put it at front-right-top, then use U R U' L' U R' U' L to cycle the other three corners around it.
Step 03
Orient the Top Corners
Now twist each corner into the correct orientation. Put a misoriented corner at front-right-top.

Do R' D' R D repeatedly (2 or 4 times) until that corner is correct, then use U to bring the next misoriented corner to front-right-top. Never move the bottom layer during this step.
Key reminder: When doing R' D' R D to orient corners, the bottom layer will look scrambled — that's normal. Keep going until the front-right-top corner is solved, then only use U to move to the next corner. The bottom layer will fix itself.
3×3 · Beginner

Layer by Layer Method

The Layer by Layer (LBL) method solves the cube one layer at a time — bottom, middle, then top. It's the best starting point for beginners and uses a small set of repeatable algorithms.

Step 01
White Cross
Start by making a white cross on the bottom. Find each white edge piece and bring it to its correct position — the edge's side color must match the center below it.

This step is done intuitively. Most people can figure it out without algorithms. Aim for all 4 white edges correctly placed.
Step 02
White Corners (First Layer)
Find each white corner piece. Bring it above its correct slot (front-right-top position). Use R U R' U' repeatedly until it drops into place.

If a white corner is stuck in the bottom layer in the wrong spot, do R U R' to pop it out first.
Step 03
Middle Layer Edges
Flip the cube so white is on the bottom. Find non-yellow edge pieces on the top layer. Match the front color of the edge to the front center, then use:

Goes Right: U R U' R' U' F' U F
Goes Left: U' L' U L U F U' F'
Step 04
Yellow Cross (Top)
Make a yellow cross on the top. You'll see one of three patterns — dot, L-shape, or line. Apply F R U R' U' F' until you get the yellow cross. You may need to apply it 1–3 times.
Step 05
Orient Yellow Edges
Now align the yellow cross edges with the side centers. Use R U R' U R U2 R' to cycle edges. You may need to apply it facing different directions.
Step 06
Position Yellow Corners
Get all 4 top corners into their correct positions (matching side colors) even if they're twisted. Hold a correctly positioned corner at front-right-top and apply U R U' L' U R' U' L to cycle the others.
Step 07
Orient Yellow Corners
Put a misoriented corner at front-right-top. Apply R' D' R D two or four times until it's correct. Use only U to go to the next corner. Never move D or the bottom layer between corners.
3×3 · CFOP

CFOP Overview

CFOP (Cross, F2L, OLL, PLL) is the most widely used speedcubing method in the world. Top solvers achieve sub-5 second solves with it. It has 4 stages, each building on the last.

Stage 01
Cross
Solve 4 edge pieces to form a cross on the bottom face. Unlike beginner method, you plan the cross in your head during inspection (you get 15 seconds before the timer starts in competition). A good cross takes 6–8 moves and sets up F2L pairs efficiently.
Stage 02
First Two Layers (F2L)
Insert all 4 corner-edge pairs into the first two layers simultaneously. Each pair consists of one corner and one edge. In beginner method these are separate steps — in CFOP you do them together, cutting ~30 moves to ~25. Intuitive F2L is learned by understanding how pieces move rather than memorising algorithms.
Stage 03
Orientation of Last Layer (OLL)
Make all top face stickers the same color in one step. There are 57 OLL cases (plus the skip). 2-Look OLL reduces this to 9 algorithms by doing it in two passes — first orient edges, then orient corners.
Stage 04
Permutation of Last Layer (PLL)
Permute all last layer pieces to their correct positions in one step. There are 21 PLL cases. 2-Look PLL reduces this to 6 algorithms — first permute corners, then permute edges.
Recommended learning path: Start with 2-Look OLL + 2-Look PLL (15 algorithms total). Once comfortable, learn full PLL (21 algs), then gradually add full OLL (57 algs). F2L becomes intuitive with practice — no algorithms needed.
3×3 · CFOP

The Cross

The cross is the foundation of CFOP. A fast, efficient cross sets up smooth F2L pairs and can make the difference between a 15-second and a 10-second solve.

Concept 01
What Makes a Good Cross
A solved cross has 4 white edges in the bottom layer, each matching the adjacent center color. The goal is to achieve this in 6–8 moves or fewer. Most beginners take 12–15 moves — cutting this down is one of the biggest early gains.

Color neutrality: Advanced solvers can solve the cross on any color. Start with white, then consider learning yellow too.
Concept 02
Planning During Inspection
In competition you get 15 seconds to inspect the cube before starting. Use this to find where all 4 white edges are and plan the cross completely.

Practice tip: Solve the cross with your eyes closed after planning. If you can do it, your plan was solid.
Concept 03
Daisy Method (for beginners)
If planning ahead feels hard, use the daisy: first bring all 4 white edges to the top around the yellow center (forming a daisy flower), then flip each one down to its correct position with a U alignment and F2 (or equivalent).
Concept 04
F2L Cross Connection
When solving the cross, think one step ahead — try to leave F2L pieces in positions that will be easy to pair up. A cross that takes 8 moves but leaves 3 F2L pairs ready is better than a 5-move cross that scrambles everything.
3×3 · CFOP

Intuitive F2L

First Two Layers (F2L) inserts 4 corner-edge pairs into the bottom two layers simultaneously. Rather than memorising 41 algorithms, you learn to understand how pieces move — this is called intuitive F2L.

Concept 01
The Pair Concept
Each F2L slot needs a corner and an edge that share a color. For example, the front-right slot needs the white-red-green corner and the red-green edge.

Your job is to: 1) Get the corner and edge to the top layer. 2) Connect them into a pair. 3) Insert the pair into the correct slot.
Concept 02
Basic Insertion
The most common F2L case: corner on top with white facing up, edge on top with non-white facing front.

Align the edge to the front, align the corner above its slot, then:
Right slot: U R U' R'
Left slot: U' L' U L

These are the same sexy moves you used in beginner method!
Concept 03
Solving on the Fly
When pieces are in awkward positions, break the problem down:

Edge or corner stuck in a slot: Do a quick insertion to kick it out without messing up the cross.
Corner in slot but wrong orientation: R U R' to pop it to the top.
Both pieces in top but misaligned: Use U moves to set them up, then connect.
Concept 04
Look Ahead
The biggest F2L skill is look-ahead — tracking where the next pair is while inserting the current one. This is what separates sub-20 from sub-15 solvers.

Practice slowly at first. Deliberately pause between pairs to locate the next one. Gradually reduce the pause. Eventually you track pieces while your hands work automatically.
Practice drill: Solve F2L with only 3 pairs, then only the 4th pair, until each becomes fluid. Then chain them together. SpeedSolving wiki has all 41 F2L cases with diagrams if you want to learn algorithmic F2L later.
3×3 · CFOP

2-Look OLL

2-Look OLL orients the last layer in two steps — first orient the edges (4 algorithms), then orient the corners (5 algorithms). Only 9 algorithms total to get OLL done. Check the Algorithms page for diagrams and moves.

Look 1
Orient the Edges
Look at the top face. Only pay attention to edge pieces (not corners). You'll see one of 4 patterns based on how many edges are pointing up:

Dot — 0 edges pointing up
Line — 2 opposite edges up
L-shape — 2 adjacent edges up
Cross — all 4 edges up (done!)

Go to the Algorithms page → 2-Look OLL → Edge Orientation to see the 3 algorithms for each case.
Look 2
Orient the Corners
Now edges are oriented. Look at only the corners. You'll see one of 7 patterns. 2-Look OLL uses the Sune and antisune algorithms to handle all of them in at most 2 applications:

Sune: R U R' U R U2 R'
Anti-Sune: R U2 R' U' R U' R'

Check the Algorithms page for all 5 corner orientation cases.
Recognition tip: For corner orientation, look at the top-right-front corner first and identify which way it needs to twist. This tells you which case you're in.
3×3 · CFOP

2-Look PLL

2-Look PLL permutes the last layer in two steps — first corners (2 algorithms), then edges (4 algorithms). Only 6 algorithms to finish the cube after OLL.

Look 1
Permute the Corners
Look at the top layer corners and find one that's already in its correct position (matches both adjacent centers). There will always be 0 or 1 such corner.

1 correct corner: Hold it at front-right-top and apply the A-perm.
0 correct corners: Apply the E-perm (or apply A-perm from any angle, then re-check).

Check the Algorithms page → 2-Look PLL → Corner Permutation.
Look 2
Permute the Edges
Corners are now solved. Use U moves to find a solved edge (one that matches its center). There will be 0 or 1.

1 solved edge: Hold it at the back. Apply U-perm (clockwise or anticlockwise) or Z-perm depending on pattern.
0 solved edges: Apply H-perm.

Check the Algorithms page → 2-Look PLL → Edge Permutation.
After PLL: Do a final U adjustment (AUF — Adjust U Face) to fully align the top layer with the sides. The cube is solved!
3×3 · Advanced

Full OLL (57 Cases)

Full OLL solves the last layer orientation in one look. There are 57 cases. Learning all of them drops your OLL time significantly and removes the second look entirely. They are all listed in the Algorithms section.

Learning Strategy
How to Approach Full OLL
Don't try to learn all 57 at once. Start with the cases you see most often. A recommended order:

1. OLL 21 (cross skip) — free!
2. Sune family (OLL 26, 27, 33, 45) — variations of Sune
3. C-shapes (OLL 34, 46)
4. T-shapes (OLL 33, 45)
5. Dot cases (OLL 1–4) — hardest, save for last

Add 2–3 new cases per week. Recognition gets faster as patterns become familiar.
Recognition system: Most solvers look at the front and right faces to identify OLL cases. Learning to recognize cases from different angles (COLL, ZBLL) is an advanced skill — focus on standard recognition first.
3×3 · Advanced

Full PLL (21 Cases)

Full PLL permutes the last layer in one algorithm. There are 21 cases (including the PLL skip). Learning all of them is one of the highest return investments in speedcubing — most are fast and finger-trick-friendly.

Learning Strategy
Priority Order for Full PLL
If you already know 2-Look PLL, you're 6 down. Recommended next cases:

High priority (fast, common): T-perm, Y-perm, F-perm, J-perms (Ja, Jb), R-perms (Ra, Rb)
Medium: G-perms (Ga–Gd), V-perm, N-perms
Lower priority (rare): E-perm, Na/Nb perms

All algorithms are in the Algorithms section with finger trick notes.
AUF recognition: Before applying PLL, use U moves to find the best angle. Some cases have a "best angle" that makes the algorithm faster due to finger tricks. Check the alg notes.
4×4 · Beginner

Reduction Method

The reduction method turns a 4×4 into a 3×3 by first solving the centers, then pairing up the edges, then solving it like a 3×3. It's the standard approach for 4×4 through 7×7.

Phase 01
Solve the Centers
The 4×4 has 4 center pieces per face (in a 2×2 arrangement). Unlike 3×3, they can move. Solve all 6 center groups — 4 pieces of the same color each forming a 2×2 block.

Start with white and yellow (opposite faces), then solve the 4 side centers. Use r (inner right slice) moves to move centers without disturbing already-solved ones.
Phase 02
Pair the Edges
Each edge on a 4×4 has 2 pieces that need to be paired together into a "dedge" (double edge). There are 12 dedges to pair.

Use the slice-flip-unslice method: bring two matching edge pieces next to each other in the top layer, use r U2 r' to pair them, then fix any center disruption. Save the last two edges for a special pairing that preserves centers.
Phase 03
Solve Like a 3×3
Once centers are solved and all edges are paired, treat the cube as a 3×3 and apply your normal method (beginner or CFOP).

Watch out for parity! 4×4 has two parity cases that can't occur on a 3×3. See the Parity Cases lesson.
4×4 · Parity

Parity Cases

Parity cases look like impossible situations on a 3×3 but can occur on a 4×4. There are two types — OLL parity and PLL parity.

Parity 01
OLL Parity
OLL parity looks like a single edge flipped on the last layer — impossible on a 3×3 but common on 4×4. It occurs roughly 50% of solves.

Algorithm: r2 B2 U2 l U2 r' U2 r U2 F2 r F2 l' B2 r2

Apply this when you see a single flipped edge during OLL. It will fix the flip and you can continue normally.
Parity 02
PLL Parity
PLL parity looks like two adjacent edges swapped — also impossible on 3×3. Occurs roughly 25% of solves.

Algorithm: r2 U2 r2 Uw2 r2 Uw2

Apply when you see two adjacent last-layer edges that need to swap. After applying, finish PLL normally.
Prevention: Some 4×4 methods (like Yau) are structured to eliminate OLL parity entirely. Worth looking into once you're comfortable with standard reduction.
5×5 – 7×7 · Method

Big Cube Reduction

5×5, 6×6, and 7×7 all use the same reduction approach as 4×4 — solve centers, pair edges, solve like 3×3. The centers and edges are just larger.

Phase 01
Centers (Larger Grids)
On a 5×5, centers are 3×3 grids (9 pieces each). On 6×6 they're 4×4 (16 pieces). On 7×7 they're 5×5 (25 pieces).

Build each center column by column or row by row. For odd-layered cubes (5×5, 7×7) the true center piece is fixed — use it as your reference. For even-layered cubes (6×6) there's no fixed center so pay attention to color matching.
Phase 02
Edges (Multiple Pieces Per Dedge)
On 5×5 each dedge has 3 pieces, on 6×6 it has 4, on 7×7 it has 5. Pair them the same way — bring matching pieces together and use slice moves to pair without disturbing centers.

The last two edges still require a special pairing move. This is the same concept as 4×4 regardless of cube size.
Phase 03
3×3 Solve + Parity
Once reduced, solve like a 3×3. On 5×5 and 7×7 (odd cubes) OLL parity is possible. On all big cubes PLL parity is possible.

The same parity algorithms from 4×4 apply — adjust slice notation to match your cube size (e.g. on 5×5 use 2r instead of r).
5×5 – 7×7 · Parity

Parity & Edge Fixing

The same two parity cases from 4×4 apply to all big cubes. Additionally, you may encounter "wing" edge mismatches during the edge pairing phase.

Issue 01
Flipped Wing Edge During Pairing
Sometimes a wing edge piece appears to be flipped. This is not true parity — it means you've made a mistake during center solving or edge pairing.

Double-check your centers are correct. If they are, the wing edge needs to be broken up and re-paired using a setup move.
Issue 02
OLL and PLL Parity on Big Cubes
Same algorithms as 4×4, but use the appropriate inner slice notation for your cube. On 5×5, replace r with 2r. On 6×6 with 3r for the innermost slice, etc.

Most big cube solvers memorize the 4×4 parity algs and just adapt the slice notation mentally.

R U2 R' U' R U' R'

R U R' U R U' R' U R U2 R'

PLL — Corner Permutation

Step 1 of 2-Look PLL. Permute the 4 top corners to their correct positions.

A-Perm (Aa)
x R' U R' D2 R U' R' D2 R2 x'
3-cycle of corners: front-right-top goes to back-right-top. Hold solved corner at front-left.
A-Perm (Ab)
x R2 D2 R U R' D2 R U' R x'
Mirror of Aa — cycle goes the other way. Hold solved corner at front-right.
E-Perm
x' R U' R' D R U R' D' R U R' D R U' R' D' x
Diagonal corner swap — no correct corners. Apply from any angle.
PLL — Edge Permutation

Step 2 of 2-Look PLL. Corners are solved. Permute the 4 top edges.

U-Perm (Ua)
R U' R U R U R U' R' U' R2
3-cycle of edges anticlockwise. Hold solved edge at back.
U-Perm (Ub)
R2 U R U R' U' R' U' R' U R'
3-cycle of edges clockwise. Mirror of Ua.
H-Perm
M2 U M2 U2 M2 U M2
Swap opposite edges. No solved edges. Apply from any angle.
Z-Perm
M2 U M2 U M' U2 M2 U2 M'
Swap adjacent edges. No solved edges. Hold so unsolved edges are front and right.
